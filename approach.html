How I approach my work

My work builds on ideas from a number of fields, with strong roots in human computer interaction, human-centered design, history of science, and the learning sciences.

In many non-Western traditions, knowledge is often described in terms of linneage: I learned from X who learned from Y. My work draws on a few lineages:

Constructionism

I'm most influenced by my mentors Mitchel Resnick (inventor of Scratch and LEGO Robotics) and Bakhtiar Mikhak, both of whom build on ideas from Seymour Papert. Papert escaped apartheid South Africa to work with Jean Piaget and later joined MIT where he collaborated closely with Marvin Minsky in founding the Artificial Intelligence Laboratory and the MIT Media Lab.

Papert introduced a set of powerful ideas for thinking about learning known as Constructionism: people learn by constructing ideas in their minds (constructivism), a process that is especially impactful when they construct ideas through external artifacts. His work intsercts with ideas from Maria Montessori, Loris Mallaguzi (Reggio Emilia), and Eleanor Duckworth (known for her legendary work supporting children in discovering wonderful ideas).

Deeper Learning

I'm also influenced by work from High Tech High, Transcend, and the Deeper Learning Network -- and in turn from mentors including Rob Riordan, Jal Mehta, and Sarah Fine.

Human Computer Interaction

This is a super broad field, one that I was introduced to recently at MIT. I'm particularly inspired by people like older hands like Alan Kay or Doug Engelbert and more recent efforts by folks at Ink and Switch, Dynamicland, HARC, the Software Design Group, and my chums at the Media Lab.

I've also recently discovered folks working on 'tools for thought'. People like Andy Matuschak or Roam Research.

Reggio and Montessori

People often point to IB (International Baccalaureate) as one of the few post-industrial school models that has reached significant scale. However, I find more inspiration in Reggio Emilia and Montessori traditions. They differ in important ways, but are alike in that they offer compelling alternatives to industrial school.

Tensions

Honest, precise conversation: Too often, discussions about education are cast in superficial, binary terms: charter or public schools, content or skills, citizenship or employment. While these tensions can be real, they often obscure deeper, causal factors ranging from resource allocation to the theories of knowledge, learning, and teaching that often underlie different models for school. For example, a project-based learning experience at High Tech High differs dramatically from a project-based experience at KIPP. However, we rarely have honest and direct conversations about the differences in these approaches.
